When most people hear "estate planning," they picture mansions, trust funds, and family dynasties. The reality is much simpler — and much more urgent. If you have children, a home, a bank account, or even just strong feelings about your own medical care, you need an estate plan.

Here is why it matters for everyday Michigan families.

## What Happens Without a Plan

If you pass away without a will in Michigan, the state decides what happens to everything you own. Under Michigan's intestacy laws (MCL 700.2101–2114), your assets are distributed according to a rigid formula:

- **If you have a spouse and children who are also your spouse's children:** Your spouse gets the first $150,000 plus half of the remainder. Your children split the rest.
- **If you have children from a different relationship:** Your spouse gets the first $150,000 plus half of the balance. Children from outside the marriage share the other half.
- **If you have no spouse:** Everything goes to your children equally. If no children, it passes to parents, then siblings, then more distant relatives.

This formula may not reflect your wishes at all. More importantly:

- **No will = no say in guardianship.** The court appoints a guardian for your minor children. It may not be the person you would choose.
- **No will = probate court involvement.** Your family must go through a public, time-consuming, and expensive court process.
- **No will = no protection for blended families.** Step-children inherit nothing unless legally adopted.

## The Core Documents Every Family Needs

### 1. Last Will and Testament

Your will states who receives your property, who serves as personal representative (executor), and — critically — who you want to raise your children. Without it, a judge makes these decisions.

### 2. Durable Power of Attorney

This document names someone to handle your financial affairs if you become incapacitated. Without it, your family must petition the court for conservatorship — an expensive, slow process that happens at the worst possible time.

### 3. Patient Advocate Designation (Medical Power of Attorney)

Michigan law allows you to name a patient advocate who can make healthcare decisions on your behalf if you cannot communicate. This includes decisions about surgery, medications, and end-of-life care.

### 4. HIPAA Authorization

Without this, your family members cannot access your medical records or speak with your doctors, even in an emergency. A simple HIPAA release solves this.

### 5. Revocable Living Trust (Optional but Powerful)

A trust is not just for the wealthy. For Michigan homeowners, a revocable living trust:
- Avoids probate entirely (saving time and money)
- Keeps your affairs private (wills become public record)
- Provides seamless management if you become incapacitated
- Can protect assets for minor children until they reach an age you choose

## Common Myths That Keep Families Unprotected

**"I'm too young."** Accidents and sudden illness do not check your age. If you have dependents, you need a plan.

**"My spouse will get everything anyway."** Not necessarily — see the intestacy rules above. And what happens if you both pass together?

**"I don't have enough assets to worry about."** Your "estate" includes your home equity, retirement accounts, life insurance, vehicles, and personal property. For most families, this adds up to more than they realize.

**"I can do it with an online template."** Generic forms miss Michigan-specific requirements. A will that is improperly witnessed, for example, can be invalidated entirely. Michigan requires two witnesses who are not beneficiaries (MCL 700.2502).

## Life Events That Should Trigger a Plan Update

Even if you already have an estate plan, certain events require a review:

- Marriage or divorce
- Birth or adoption of a child
- Purchase of a home
- Significant change in assets
- Death of a named beneficiary or executor
- Moving to or from Michigan (laws vary by state)
- Changes in tax law

## The Cost of Waiting

We have seen families spend $15,000–$30,000 in probate costs and legal fees that could have been avoided with a $1,500 estate plan. Beyond money, the emotional cost of family disputes over who gets what — or who raises the children — is immeasurable.

## Getting Started Is Easier Than You Think

A basic estate plan for a Michigan family can typically be completed in one to two meetings with an attorney. You will need to think about:

- Who should raise your children if you cannot
- Who you trust to manage finances on your behalf
- How you want your assets distributed
- What kind of medical care you do (or do not) want in a crisis
