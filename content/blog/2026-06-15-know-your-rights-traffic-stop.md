Being pulled over by the police can be stressful, even if you have done nothing wrong. Knowing your rights ahead of time makes the experience less intimidating and helps protect you legally if the stop leads to a citation — or worse.

## What to Do When You See Those Lights

**Pull over safely and quickly.** Signal, slow down, and find a well-lit area if possible. Turn off your engine, roll your window down, and keep your hands visible on the steering wheel. Officers appreciate being able to see that you are not a threat.

**Be polite but brief.** You are required to provide your driver's license, vehicle registration, and proof of insurance when asked. Beyond that, you are not obligated to answer questions about where you are going, where you have been, or whether you have been drinking.

## Your Rights During the Stop

### You Can Remain Silent

Under the Fifth Amendment, you do not have to answer incriminating questions. A simple "I'd prefer not to answer that" is sufficient. You cannot be penalized for exercising this right.

### You Can Refuse a Search

If an officer asks to search your vehicle, you have the right to say no. However, if the officer has probable cause — for example, they see contraband in plain view — they may search without your consent. Clearly and calmly state: "I do not consent to a search."

### Field Sobriety Tests Are Voluntary

In Michigan, you are not legally required to perform roadside field sobriety tests (walk-and-turn, one-leg-stand, etc.). However, refusing a chemical test (breath, blood, or urine) after arrest triggers Michigan's Implied Consent law, which can result in an automatic one-year license suspension.

### You Can Record the Encounter

Michigan is a one-party consent state. You have the right to record your interaction with the officer as long as you do not physically interfere with their duties.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- **Arguing on the roadside.** The side of the road is not a courtroom. If you believe the stop was unlawful, challenge it later with your attorney.
- **Reaching for items before being asked.** Wait until the officer requests your documents, then tell them where the items are before reaching.
- **Admitting fault.** Saying "I know I was speeding" becomes evidence. Let the officer state their reason for the stop.
- **Ignoring the ticket.** Failing to respond to a citation can result in a bench warrant, license suspension, and additional fines.

## What Happens After the Stop

If you receive a citation, you typically have 10 to 14 days to respond. You can pay the fine (which is an admission of guilt), request an informal hearing, or request a formal hearing where you can be represented by an attorney.

For many moving violations, hiring an attorney can result in reduced charges, fewer points on your record, and lower insurance impacts. This is especially true for CDL holders, drivers under 18, or anyone facing a suspension.

## When to Call a Lawyer

If your traffic stop resulted in an arrest, a vehicle search, or charges beyond a simple civil infraction, you should speak with an attorney immediately. The decisions you make in the first 24 to 48 hours can significantly impact the outcome of your case.
