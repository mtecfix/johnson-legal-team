The moments after a car accident are chaotic. Your adrenaline is high, you may be in pain, and you are likely unsure what to do next. But the actions you take in the first hours and days after a crash can make or break a future injury claim.

Here is a step-by-step guide for Michigan drivers.

## At the Scene

### 1. Check for Injuries and Call 911

Your safety comes first. Check yourself and your passengers for injuries. Even if the accident seems minor, call 911 — a police report is valuable evidence, and some injuries (like whiplash or concussions) do not show symptoms immediately.

### 2. Move to Safety If Possible

If your vehicle is drivable and you are blocking traffic, Michigan law allows you to move to the shoulder. Turn on your hazard lights.

### 3. Exchange Information

Get the following from all involved drivers:
- Full name and contact information
- Insurance company and policy number
- Driver's license number
- License plate number
- Vehicle make, model, and color

### 4. Document Everything

Use your phone to photograph:
- Damage to all vehicles (close-up and wide shots)
- The overall accident scene (road conditions, traffic signs, skid marks)
- Your injuries (bruises, cuts, swelling)
- The other driver's license plate and insurance card

### 5. Talk to Witnesses

If anyone saw the accident, get their name and phone number. Witness statements can be critical if fault is disputed.

### 6. Do NOT Admit Fault

Even a simple "I'm sorry" can be used against you later. Stick to the facts when speaking with the other driver and the police. Let the investigation determine fault.

## In the Days After

### 7. Seek Medical Attention Within 24–48 Hours

Even if you feel fine, see a doctor. Many accident injuries — herniated discs, soft tissue damage, traumatic brain injuries — have delayed onset. A medical record linking your injuries to the crash is essential for any future claim.

### 8. Notify Your Insurance Company

Michigan is a no-fault state, meaning your own insurance pays for your medical bills and lost wages regardless of who caused the accident. Report the crash to your insurer promptly, but keep your statement factual and brief. You are not required to give a recorded statement to the other driver's insurer.

### 9. Keep a Recovery Journal

Document your daily pain levels, limitations, missed work, and how the injuries affect your routine. This record supports your claim for pain and suffering damages.

### 10. Consult an Attorney Before Accepting Any Settlement

Insurance companies — including your own — are businesses. Their goal is to close claims quickly and cheaply. Before signing anything or accepting a check, have an attorney review your case.

## Understanding Michigan's No-Fault System

Michigan's no-fault insurance system is unique and often confusing:

- **Personal Injury Protection (PIP):** Your insurer covers your medical expenses, up to your policy limit, regardless of fault.
- **Mini-tort claims:** If the other driver was at fault, you can recover up to $3,000 for vehicle damage not covered by your policy.
- **Third-party lawsuits:** You can sue the at-fault driver for pain and suffering only if you suffered a "serious impairment of body function" or permanent disfigurement.

The threshold for filing a third-party lawsuit is fact-specific and often contested by insurance companies. An experienced personal injury attorney can evaluate whether your injuries meet Michigan's legal standard.

## When You Need a Lawyer

Not every fender-bender requires an attorney. But you should consult one if:

- You have injuries requiring ongoing medical treatment
- You missed work or cannot return to your job
- The insurance company is delaying or denying your PIP benefits
- You believe the other driver was at fault and your injuries are serious
- A commercial vehicle (truck, bus, rideshare) was involved
- The other driver was uninsured or underinsured

## Time Limits Matter

Michigan has strict deadlines for accident-related claims:
- **PIP benefits:** Must notify your insurer within 1 year of the accident
- **Third-party lawsuits:** 3-year statute of limitations from the date of the crash
- **Mini-tort claims:** 1 year from the date of the accident

Missing these deadlines means losing your right to compensation entirely.
