When you are charged with a crime in Michigan, one of the first things to understand is whether the charge is a misdemeanor or a felony. This classification affects everything — from where your case is heard to the maximum punishment you face and how the conviction impacts your life long-term.

## The Basic Distinction

In Michigan, the dividing line is straightforward:

- **Misdemeanor:** A crime punishable by up to 1 year in the county jail.
- **Felony:** A crime punishable by more than 1 year in state prison.

But the real-world consequences of each go far beyond jail time.

## Types of Misdemeanors in Michigan

Michigan misdemeanors are generally classified by maximum penalty:

| Classification | Max Jail Time | Max Fine | Example Offenses |
|---|---|---|---|
| 93-day misdemeanor | 93 days | $500 | Disorderly conduct, trespassing |
| 1-year misdemeanor | 1 year | $1,000 | Assault & battery, retail fraud (1st offense under $200) |
| High-court misdemeanor | Up to 2 years | $2,000+ | Indecent exposure, OWI (2nd offense) |

**Common misdemeanor charges we handle:**
- Operating While Intoxicated (OWI/DUI) — first offense
- Domestic assault
- Retail fraud / shoplifting
- Driving on a suspended license
- Disorderly conduct
- Minor in possession (MIP)

## Types of Felonies in Michigan

Felonies range from relatively low-level to life offenses:

| Class | Max Sentence | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| Class A | Life | First-degree murder, CSC 1st degree |
| Class B | 20 years | Armed robbery, assault with intent to murder |
| Class C | 15 years | Manslaughter, CSC 2nd degree |
| Class D | 10 years | Embezzlement over $100K, home invasion 2nd degree |
| Class E | 5 years | Felonious assault, receiving stolen property over $1,000 |
| Class F | 4 years | Retail fraud 1st degree, OWI 3rd offense |
| Class G | 2 years | Larceny $1,000–$20,000 |
| Class H | Variable | Catch-all for unclassified felonies |

## Why a "Minor" Misdemeanor Still Matters

Many people assume a misdemeanor is no big deal — pay a fine, do some community service, and move on. In reality, a misdemeanor conviction creates lasting consequences:

### Employment
Employers routinely run background checks. A misdemeanor conviction for theft, violence, or intoxication can disqualify you from jobs in healthcare, education, finance, and government — even years later.

### Housing
Landlords use criminal background checks to screen tenants. A conviction can mean denied applications, especially in competitive rental markets.

### Professional Licensing
If you hold or plan to obtain a professional license (nursing, teaching, real estate, CDL), a misdemeanor conviction triggers review and potential denial or revocation.

### Immigration
For non-citizens, even a misdemeanor can trigger deportation proceedings, denial of naturalization, or visa revocation. This is one area where the stakes are especially high.

### Gun Rights
Certain misdemeanors — particularly domestic violence convictions — result in a federal prohibition on firearm possession under the Lachuk amendment. This is permanent unless the conviction is expunged.

## The Critical Differences in How Cases Are Handled

### Where the Case Is Heard
- Misdemeanors are handled in District Court
- Felonies begin in District Court (for preliminary examination) but are tried in Circuit Court

### Right to a Jury
- Both carry the right to a jury trial in Michigan
- Felony juries require 12 members; misdemeanor juries require 6

### Sentencing Guidelines
- Felony sentences are governed by Michigan's sentencing guidelines, which calculate a recommended range based on the offense severity and your prior record
- Misdemeanor sentences are at the judge's discretion within statutory limits

### Probation
- Misdemeanor probation: typically up to 2 years
- Felony probation: up to 5 years (with judicial override available)

## Defense Strategies That Make a Difference

Regardless of the classification, having an experienced criminal defense attorney can lead to outcomes far better than the worst-case scenario:

**Charge reduction:** A felony reduced to a misdemeanor, or a misdemeanor reduced to a civil infraction, dramatically changes the long-term impact.

**Diversion programs:** Michigan offers programs like HYTA (Holmes Youthful Trainee Act) for defendants under 26, and various district court diversion programs that result in dismissal upon completion.

**Plea negotiation:** An attorney who knows the local prosecutors and judges can often negotiate outcomes that self-represented defendants cannot access.

**Suppression of evidence:** If your rights were violated during the investigation — illegal search, improper interrogation, flawed traffic stop — evidence can be excluded, weakening or destroying the prosecution's case.

## When to Act

If you have been charged with any criminal offense in Michigan:

1. **Do not discuss the case** with anyone except your attorney
2. **Do not post about it** on social media
3. **Show up to every court date** — failure to appear results in a bench warrant
4. **Contact a lawyer immediately** — early intervention gives your attorney the most options

The difference between a conviction and a dismissal often comes down to the quality of your defense and how quickly you act.
