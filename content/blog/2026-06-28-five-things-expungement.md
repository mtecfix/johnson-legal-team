A criminal record follows you everywhere — job applications, housing, loans, even volunteer work. But Michigan's expanded expungement laws mean more people than ever may be eligible to set aside old convictions and move forward with a clean slate.

Here are five things you should know before starting the process.

## 1. Michigan's Clean Slate Laws Have Expanded Significantly

In 2021, Michigan enacted some of the most progressive expungement reforms in the country. The Clean Slate legislation added automatic expungement for certain misdemeanors (after 7 years) and felonies (after 10 years) if no new convictions occur. For cases that do not qualify for automatic expungement, petition-based expungement was also expanded.

**Key changes include:**

- Up to 3 felonies can now be set aside (previously limited to 1)
- An unlimited number of misdemeanors can be set aside
- Marijuana offenses that would be legal today have a simplified process
- Traffic offenses resulting in injury or death may now be eligible
- "One bad night" provision allows multiple offenses from a single incident to count as one

## 2. Not Everything Is Eligible

Some convictions cannot be expunged regardless of how much time has passed:

- Life felonies (murder, criminal sexual conduct 1st degree)
- Most felonies with a maximum sentence of 10+ years involving violence
- Child abuse convictions
- Human trafficking offenses
- Certain felony domestic violence convictions

Additionally, you must have completed your entire sentence — including probation, parole, and restitution — before you can apply.

## 3. The Process Takes Time and Preparation

While the law expanded eligibility, the process itself still requires careful attention:

**Step 1: Obtain Your Criminal Record.** Request your official record from the Michigan State Police. This costs $10 and ensures you know exactly what is on your record.

**Step 2: Determine Eligibility.** Review each conviction against current eligibility rules. Waiting periods apply — typically 3 years after completing your sentence for misdemeanors, 5 years for felonies.

**Step 3: File the Application.** You must file a petition with the court that convicted you. This includes a completed MC 227 form, your fingerprints, and a filing fee (typically $50).

**Step 4: Attend the Hearing.** The prosecutor has 60 days to respond. If they object, a hearing is scheduled where you present your case for why the conviction should be set aside.

**Step 5: Wait for the Decision.** If granted, the order is sent to the Michigan State Police, FBI, and the arresting agency. The conviction will no longer appear on standard background checks.

## 4. An Attorney Significantly Improves Your Chances

While you can file for expungement on your own, having an attorney increases approval rates substantially. A lawyer can:

- Identify every eligible conviction (some people have more that qualify than they realize)
- Present compelling arguments about rehabilitation and community contribution
- Handle procedural requirements that trip up many self-filers
- Navigate objections from the prosecutor's office
- Ensure proper follow-up so the expungement is actually reflected on your record

Many expungement petitions are denied not on merit, but because of paperwork errors or missed deadlines.

## 5. A Clean Record Changes Everything

Clients who have successfully expunged their records often tell us the impact is life-changing:

- **Employment:** No more checking "yes" on job applications asking about felony convictions
- **Housing:** Pass rental background checks that previously disqualified you
- **Education:** Qualify for financial aid and professional licensing programs
- **Peace of mind:** The past no longer defines your future
